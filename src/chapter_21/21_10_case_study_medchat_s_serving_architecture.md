## 21.10 Case Study: MedChat's Serving Architecture

---

MedChat is the clinical chatbot that has appeared in several chapters of this book as a running case study. By the time MedChat reached production deployment, its serving architecture had evolved considerably from the proof-of-concept that impressed the clinical steering committee. This section traces the key decisions made in that evolution and the reasons behind them.

**The gateway.** MedChat runs all model interactions through a self-hosted LiteLLM proxy deployed as an always-on Kubernetes service. The gateway abstracts the three model providers used across MedChat's cascade: a locally hosted Mistral model on the hospital's private compute cluster, Anthropic Claude via the Anthropic API, and a frontier model reserved for the highest-complexity clinical queries. The local model is used for routing classification and handling queries that must not leave the hospital network due to data residency requirements; the cloud models handle the majority of clinical queries. By routing all traffic through the LiteLLM gateway, the engineering team has a single point for cost attribution, audit logging, and guardrail enforcement — all of which are required by the hospital's governance framework.

**The cascade.** MedChat's routing operates in three tiers. The local Mistral model receives every query and performs two functions: it classifies whether the query contains protected health information that must not be forwarded to cloud providers, and it assesses query complexity to determine the appropriate tier. Simple informational queries — appointment information, clinic hours, procedure preparation instructions — are served by a mid-tier Claude model. Complex queries involving medication reasoning, differential diagnosis, or multi-step clinical pathways escalate to the frontier tier. Escalation rate is monitored weekly; consistently high escalation rates trigger prompt optimisation or cascade threshold adjustment.

**Streaming.** MedChat streams all responses to the clinical interface. The clinical team's user research during piloting was unambiguous: clinicians waiting for a non-streaming response reported the system felt "broken" or "frozen," even when total response time was reasonable. Streaming requires MedChat's always-on deployment, since streaming connections exceed the timeout limits of available serverless platforms in the hospital's cloud environment. The LiteLLM proxy normalises streaming across all three model tiers, presenting a consistent SSE stream to the frontend regardless of which underlying model generated the response.

**Input guardrails.** MedChat's input pipeline applies three layers of input validation: a protected health information detector that intercepts any query containing patient identifiers, health record numbers, or diagnostic information that should not leave the private network; a prompt injection classifier trained on a curated dataset of healthcare-specific injection attacks; and a content classifier that flags queries outside MedChat's clinical scope for redirection rather than rejection. PHI-containing queries are routed to the local model only. Injection-classified queries are rejected with a logged alert. Out-of-scope queries receive a structured redirection response.

**Output guardrails.** MedChat's output pipeline operates on completed responses before delivery to the interface. A grounding checker compares every factual claim against the retrieved clinical guidelines used to construct the RAG context. A citation verifier confirms that any referenced guideline exists in the hospital's approved knowledge base. A safety classifier screens for content that could constitute clinical advice beyond MedChat's authorised scope — the system is designed to inform, not to diagnose or prescribe. High-risk outputs that pass the automated classifiers but contain certain clinical signal patterns are held for clinician review before delivery.

**Cost management.** MedChat's LiteLLM deployment includes per-user token quotas that reset monthly. Clinicians who approach their quota receive a soft warning; those who exceed it have their requests routed to the mid-tier model regardless of complexity classification until the quota resets. The engineering team reviews cost attribution reports weekly, examining cost per query type, escalation rates, and guardrail rejection rates. Prompt compression audits run quarterly, reviewing the system prompts for each tier and regression-testing compressed versions against the clinical evaluation dataset before deployment.

The MedChat architecture carries genuine operational cost, and clinical governance makes that cost non-negotiable. Clinical governance requirements, data residency constraints, and the consequences of guardrail failures — a hallucinated drug dosage reaching a clinician — justify an architecture that a consumer application could not support economically. The case illustrates that LLM serving architecture is always contextual: the right design depends on who bears the cost of failure, not on what is technically possible.
