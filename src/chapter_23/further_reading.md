## Further Reading

---

- Wooldridge, M., & Jennings, N. R. (1995). Intelligent agents: Theory and practice. *The Knowledge Engineering Review*, 10(2), 115–152. The foundational academic treatment of agent theory, defining agent properties, architectures, and the distinction between deliberative and reactive agents. Essential background for understanding why multi-agent coordination is an engineering problem distinct from single-agent design.

- Hong, S., Zheng, X., Chen, J., Cheng, Y., Zhang, C., Wang, Z., & Wu, C. (2023). MetaGPT: Meta programming for a multi-agent collaborative framework. *arXiv:2308.00352*. Describes a multi-agent software engineering framework that assigns structured roles — product manager, architect, engineer, QA — to agents in a collaborative pipeline, with explicit shared artefacts managing inter-agent communication. A practical example of supervisor/worker and pipeline patterns applied to software development tasks.

- Wu, Q., Bansal, G., Zhang, J., Wu, Y., Li, B., Zhu, E., & Wang, C. (2023). AutoGen: Enabling next-gen LLM applications via multi-agent conversation. *arXiv:2308.08155*. Introduces a framework for multi-agent conversation patterns, including supervisor/worker and peer-to-peer configurations, with empirical comparisons across task types. Particularly useful for understanding how termination conditions and conversation structure affect reliability.

- Commodity Futures Trading Commission & Securities and Exchange Commission. (2010). *Findings regarding the market events of May 6, 2010: Report of the staffs of the CFTC and SEC to the joint advisory committee on emerging regulatory issues*. The official joint investigation into the Flash Crash, documenting how automated trading systems interacted to produce cascading instability. The canonical real-world multi-agent failure case and essential reading for understanding emergent failure in systems of individually rational agents.

- Erman, L. D., Hayes-Roth, F., Lesser, V. R., & Reddy, D. R. (1980). The HEARSAY-II speech-understanding system: Integrating knowledge to resolve uncertainty. *ACM Computing Surveys*, 12(2), 213–253. The original formulation of the blackboard architecture pattern, developed for speech understanding and subsequently generalised to multi-agent knowledge integration. Demonstrates the pattern's strengths and the consistency challenges that remain relevant in contemporary implementations.

- Fowler, M. (2002). *Patterns of Enterprise Application Architecture*. Addison-Wesley. The chapters on concurrency patterns, event sourcing, and circuit breakers provide the distributed systems engineering foundation on which multi-agent containment strategies are built. Circuit breakers are covered extensively in the companion book *Release It!* by Michael Nygard (Pragmatic Bookshelf, 2018), which is equally recommended.
