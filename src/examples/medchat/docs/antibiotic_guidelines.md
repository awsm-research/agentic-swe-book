## 1. Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

### 1.1 Definition and Diagnosis

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is defined as an acute lower respiratory tract
infection acquired outside a hospital setting, presenting with symptoms of pneumonia
(cough, fever, rigors, pleuritic chest pain, dyspnoea) together with a new infiltrate
on chest radiograph.

Severity should be assessed using the CRB-65 score:
- **Confusion** (new onset)
- **Respiratory rate** ≥ 30 breaths/min
- **Blood pressure** systolic < 90 mmHg or diastolic ≤ 60 mmHg
- **Age** ≥ 65 years

Score 0: Low severity — consider home treatment.
Score 1–2: Moderate severity — hospital assessment advised.
Score 3–4: High severity — urgent hospitalisation required.

### 1.2 First-Line Treatment — Mild CAP (CRB-65 score 0)

**First-line (community-managed):**
- **Amoxicillin** 500 mg orally three times daily (TDS) for 5 days

**Penicillin-allergic patients:**
- **Doxycycline** 200 mg orally as a loading dose, then 100 mg twice daily (BD)
  for 5 days (total course)
- Alternatively: **Clarithromycin** 500 mg orally BD for 5 days

**Atypical cover required** (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydophila — consider if
not responding within 48 h or if epidemiological risk):
- Add **Clarithromycin** 500 mg orally BD to the amoxicillin regimen, or
- Use **Doxycycline** alone (covers both typical and atypical pathogens)

### 1.3 Moderate-to-Severe CAP (CRB-65 score 1–2, hospitalised)

**First-line:**
- **Co-amoxiclav** (amoxicillin/clavulanate) 1.2 g IV every 8 hours
  PLUS
- **Clarithromycin** 500 mg IV/orally BD

**Penicillin-allergic patients:**
- **Levofloxacin** 500 mg orally/IV once daily for 7 days
  (requires local approval; risk of QTc prolongation — check baseline ECG)

**Duration:** 5–7 days for moderate CAP; extend to 7–10 days if Legionella
confirmed or poor initial response.

### 1.4 Severe CAP (CRB-65 score 3–4, ICU admission)

**First-line:**
- **Co-amoxiclav** 1.2 g IV every 8 hours
  PLUS
- **Clarithromycin** 500 mg IV BD
  OR
- **Levofloxacin** 500 mg IV BD (if penicillin allergy or Legionella suspected)

**Add MRSA cover if risk factors present** (prior MRSA colonisation, recent
hospitalisation, residence in long-term care):
- **Vancomycin** IV (dose by weight and renal function; target trough 15–20 mg/L)
  OR
- **Linezolid** 600 mg IV/orally BD

**Dosing table — IV to oral switch criteria:**
| Criterion | Required |
|---|---|
| Afebrile for ≥ 24 hours | Yes |
| Heart rate < 100 bpm | Yes |
| Respiratory rate < 24/min | Yes |
| Systolic BP ≥ 90 mmHg | Yes |
| SpO₂ ≥ 94% on air | Yes |
| Tolerating oral intake | Yes |

## 2. Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia (HAP)

### 2.1 Definition

Hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP) is defined as pneumonia occurring ≥ 48 hours after
hospital admission, not present or incubating at the time of admission.
Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) is a subtype occurring ≥ 48 hours after
endotracheal intubation.

### 2.2 Empirical Treatment

**Non-severe HAP (no ICU, no risk factors for resistant organisms):**
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** (Tazocin) 4.5 g IV every 8 hours
  OR
- **Co-amoxiclav** 1.2 g IV every 8 hours (if lower resistance risk)

**Severe HAP / VAP (ICU-level care, or prior resistant organism):**
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5 g IV every 6 hours (extended infusion if
  available)
  OR
- **Meropenem** 1 g IV every 8 hours (reserve for documented ESBL-producing
  Gram-negatives or piperacillin-tazobactam allergy)

**MRSA coverage — add when:**
- Prior MRSA colonisation or infection documented
- Purulent sputum with Gram-positive cocci in clusters
- Risk factors: recent broad-spectrum antibiotics, haemodialysis, prior ICU admission

**MRSA treatment:**
- **Vancomycin** IV — weight-based dosing, target AUC/MIC 400–600 mg·h/L
  (or trough 15–20 mg/L if AUC monitoring unavailable)
  OR
- **Linezolid** 600 mg IV/orally BD (preferred if renal impairment limits
  vancomycin use)

**Duration:** 7 days for non-severe HAP; 8 days for VAP (14 days if
Pseudomonas aeruginosa or Acinetobacter baumannii confirmed).

### 2.3 De-escalation Principles

Review culture results at 48–72 hours. De-escalate from broad-spectrum empirical
therapy to targeted therapy based on sensitivity data:
- If Gram-positive only: stop Gram-negative cover
- If MRSA not confirmed: stop vancomycin/linezolid
- If ESBL not confirmed: switch meropenem to narrower-spectrum agent

## 3. Urinary Tract Infections (UTI)

### 3.1 Uncomplicated Lower UTI (Cystitis) — Non-pregnant Adult Women

**First-line:**
- **Nitrofurantoin** 100 mg modified-release orally BD for 5 days
  (contraindicated if eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73 m²)

**Second-line / trimethoprim alternatives:**
- **Trimethoprim** 200 mg orally BD for 7 days
  (avoid if local resistance > 20% or if used within previous 3 months)

**Pivmecillinam** 400 mg orally BD for 3–7 days is a suitable alternative
with low resistance rates.

**Fosfomycin** 3 g orally as a single dose is appropriate where local
resistance data support its use.

### 3.2 Complicated UTI and Pyelonephritis

**Mild-to-moderate pyelonephritis (outpatient-suitable):**
- **Cefalexin** 500 mg orally QDS (four times daily) for 7–14 days
  OR
- **Co-amoxiclav** 625 mg orally TDS for 7–14 days

**Severe pyelonephritis / complicated UTI requiring hospitalisation:**
- **Gentamicin** IV once daily (dose by weight; monitor levels)
  OR
- **Ceftriaxone** 2 g IV once daily
- Step down to oral agent at 48–72 hours once improving and sensitivities available

**Duration:** 7 days total for uncomplicated pyelonephritis; 10–14 days for
complicated UTI (structural abnormality, renal calculi, indwelling catheter).

### 3.3 Catheter-Associated UTI (CAUTI)

Treat only if symptomatic (fever, rigors, flank pain, altered consciousness) —
bacteriuria without symptoms does not require antibiotic treatment in catheterised
patients. Remove or replace catheter before starting antibiotics where possible.

**Empirical treatment:**
- **Nitrofurantoin** (avoid — inadequate tissue levels)
- **Co-amoxiclav** 625 mg orally TDS, or **Ciprofloxacin** 500 mg orally BD
- Adjust based on urine culture sensitivities

## 4. Skin and Soft Tissue Infections (SSTI)

### 4.1 Cellulitis and Erysipelas

**Mild (non-severe, no systemic features):**
- **Flucloxacillin** 500 mg orally QDS for 5–7 days
  (first-line for Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus pyogenes)

**Penicillin-allergic patients:**
- **Clarithromycin** 500 mg orally BD for 5–7 days
  OR
- **Clindamycin** 300 mg orally TDS for 5–7 days

**Moderate (systemic features: fever, raised CRP/WBC, rapid spreading):**
- **Flucloxacillin** 1–2 g IV QDS
  Oral step-down when afebrile ≥ 24 h and inflammation receding

**Erysipelas** (superficial, well-demarcated, lymphatic involvement):
- Predominantly Streptococcus pyogenes — **Amoxicillin** 500 mg orally TDS or
  **Phenoxymethylpenicillin** 500 mg orally QDS for 7 days is often adequate

### 4.2 Necrotising Fasciitis

Surgical emergency — urgent surgical debridement is the primary treatment.
Empirical antibiotics pending surgery and cultures:
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5 g IV every 6 hours
  PLUS
- **Clindamycin** 900 mg IV every 8 hours (antitoxin effect, inhibits toxin
  production)
  PLUS
- **Vancomycin** IV (MRSA cover pending cultures)

### 4.3 Diabetic Foot Infection

**Mild (superficial, wound < 2 cm, no systemic features):**
- **Co-amoxiclav** 625 mg orally TDS for 7–14 days

**Moderate/severe (deep tissue, osteomyelitis risk, systemic features):**
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5 g IV every 8 hours
  PLUS (if MRSA risk): **Vancomycin** IV
- Bone culture essential if osteomyelitis suspected
- Duration 4–6 weeks for confirmed osteomyelitis

## 5. Antibiotic Stewardship Principles

### 5.1 Core Principles

1. **Use antibiotics only when there is evidence of bacterial infection.** Viral
   infections (most upper respiratory tract infections, influenza) do not benefit
   from antibiotic treatment and contribute to resistance.

2. **Choose the narrowest-spectrum antibiotic effective** for the likely pathogen.
   Reserve broad-spectrum agents (carbapenems, piperacillin-tazobactam, glycopeptides)
   for infections where broader cover is genuinely needed.

3. **Duration principles:** Complete the prescribed course but do not extend
   beyond the recommended duration without clear clinical indication. Longer courses
   do not reduce resistance and increase adverse effects.

4. **Review at 48–72 hours** (the "antibiotic review" or "de-escalation opportunity"):
   - Has the clinical picture improved?
   - Are culture and sensitivity results available?
   - Can the spectrum be narrowed?
   - Can treatment be stepped down to oral?

5. **Document the indication, agent, dose, route, and planned duration** in the
   patient record at the time of prescribing. Review documentation reduces
   inadvertent prolongation of courses.

### 5.2 Monitoring Parameters by Drug Class

| Drug / Class | Key Monitoring | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Vancomycin | Renal function, AUC/MIC or trough levels | Every 48–72 h or with dose change |
| Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin) | Renal function, drug levels (pre and post) | With each dose initially; daily |
| Linezolid | FBC (thrombocytopenia, anaemia) | Weekly; earlier if prolonged course |
| Metronidazole (prolonged) | Peripheral neuropathy symptoms | Patient-reported; weekly |
| Fluoroquinolones | QTc interval | Baseline ECG; monitor with interacting drugs |
| Carbapenems | Seizure threshold in renally impaired | Clinical assessment |

### 5.3 Common Antibiotic Contraindications

| Drug | Key Contraindications |
|---|---|
| Nitrofurantoin | eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73 m² |
| Trimethoprim | Pregnancy (first trimester — folate antagonism); hyperkalaemia risk in renal impairment |
| Ciprofloxacin | QTc prolongation; tendinopathy risk (especially with corticosteroids) |
| Doxycycline | Pregnancy; children < 12 years |
| Metronidazole | First trimester pregnancy (high-dose); avoid alcohol |
| Clindamycin | Known Clostridioides difficile history |
| Chloramphenicol (systemic) | Bone marrow suppression history; neonates (grey baby syndrome) |

### 5.4 Clostridioides difficile Risk Reduction

High-risk antibiotics for C. difficile infection (CDI):
- **Highest risk:** Clindamycin, broad-spectrum cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones
- **Moderate risk:** Amoxicillin/clavulanate, ampicillin
- **Lower risk:** Metronidazole (oral), vancomycin (oral), aminoglycosides

If CDI develops: stop the precipitating antibiotic if possible. Treat with
**Fidaxomicin** 200 mg orally BD for 10 days (preferred) or
**Vancomycin** 125 mg orally QDS for 10 days.

## 6. Antibiotic Allergy and Cross-Reactivity

### 6.1 Penicillin Allergy

True IgE-mediated penicillin allergy is present in < 1% of patients who report
penicillin allergy. Documented anaphylaxis or urticaria within 1 hour warrants
caution; rash occurring days after exposure is less likely to represent true allergy.

**Cross-reactivity with cephalosporins:**
- Historical estimates of 10% cross-reactivity are outdated. Actual cross-reactivity
  is 1–2%, driven by shared R1 side chains, not the core beta-lactam ring.
- Cephalosporins with different R1 side chains (e.g., cefuroxime, ceftriaxone)
  can generally be used in penicillin-allergic patients without history of
  anaphylaxis.

**Cross-reactivity with carbapenems:**
- < 1% in patients with penicillin allergy.
- Carbapenems are generally considered safe to use in penicillin-allergic patients
  when clinically necessary.

### 6.2 Safe Alternatives by Indication

| Indication | Standard first-line | Penicillin-allergy alternative |
|---|---|---|
| CAP (mild) | Amoxicillin | Doxycycline 100 mg BD |
| CAP (moderate) | Co-amoxiclav + clarithromycin | Levofloxacin 500 mg OD |
| Cellulitis | Flucloxacillin | Clarithromycin or Clindamycin |
| UTI (uncomplicated) | Nitrofurantoin | Trimethoprim 200 mg BD |
| Pyelonephritis | Cefalexin | Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD |
