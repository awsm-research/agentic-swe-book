## Q1: When should I switch a patient from intravenous to oral antibiotics?

**A:** The decision to switch from IV to oral antibiotics (IV-to-oral switch, or IVOS)
should be made at 48–72 hours of treatment when the patient meets all of the following
criteria:

1. **Clinical improvement** — improving symptoms, decreasing inflammatory markers
   (CRP, WBC trending down).
2. **Afebrile** — temperature < 38 °C for at least 24 consecutive hours.
3. **Haemodynamically stable** — heart rate < 100 bpm, systolic BP ≥ 90 mmHg without
   vasopressor support.
4. **Tolerating oral intake** — no vomiting, functioning gastrointestinal tract.
5. **Suitable oral agent available** with good bioavailability for the identified or
   suspected organism.

Infections where IV therapy should generally continue despite clinical improvement
include: endocarditis, osteomyelitis (until surgical debridement complete), meningitis,
Staphylococcus aureus bacteraemia, and severe immunosuppression.

Early IVOS reduces catheter-related complications, facilitates earlier discharge, and
lowers costs without compromising outcomes in appropriate patients.

## Q2: What are the alternatives for patients with penicillin allergy?

**A:** The correct alternative depends on the severity of the reported penicillin
reaction and the indication:

**Low-risk allergy** (delayed maculopapular rash, no urticaria or anaphylaxis):
- Cephalosporins (e.g., cefalexin, ceftriaxone) can generally be used safely — true
  cross-reactivity is < 2% based on shared side-chain similarity, not the beta-lactam ring.
- Document the specific reaction type to guide future prescribing.

**High-risk allergy** (urticaria within 1 hour, anaphylaxis, angioedema):
- Avoid all beta-lactams unless formal allergy testing has been performed.
- Alternatives by indication:
  - **CAP:** Doxycycline 100 mg BD or Levofloxacin 500 mg OD
  - **Cellulitis:** Clarithromycin 500 mg BD or Clindamycin 300 mg TDS
  - **UTI (uncomplicated):** Nitrofurantoin 100 mg BD MR or Trimethoprim 200 mg BD
  - **Pyelonephritis:** Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD (oral) or Gentamicin IV

Always document the allergy status clearly in the patient record and refer for formal
penicillin allergy testing where feasible — the majority of patients labelled
"penicillin allergic" can safely receive penicillins after testing.

## Q3: How do I adjust antibiotic doses for patients with renal impairment?

**A:** Many antibiotics are renally cleared and require dose adjustment when eGFR
is reduced. Key principles:

**Drugs requiring significant dose reduction in renal impairment:**

| Drug | Adjustment threshold | Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| Nitrofurantoin | eGFR < 30 mL/min | **Contraindicated** (therapeutic levels not achieved; risk of peripheral neuropathy) |
| Trimethoprim | eGFR < 15 mL/min | Avoid; if essential, reduce dose and monitor potassium |
| Gentamicin | Any renal impairment | Extend dosing interval based on levels; seek pharmacy advice |
| Vancomycin | Any renal impairment | AUC-guided dosing; involve pharmacy |
| Piperacillin-tazobactam | eGFR < 20 mL/min | Reduce dose to 2.25 g every 6–8 hours |
| Fluconazole | eGFR < 50 mL/min | Reduce dose by 50% |
| Aciclovir | eGFR < 25 mL/min | Dose reduce and extend interval |
| Ciprofloxacin | eGFR < 30 mL/min | Reduce dose by 50% |

**Drugs that generally do NOT require dose adjustment in renal impairment:**
- Amoxicillin (mild-moderate impairment), Clindamycin, Metronidazole, Linezolid,
  Moxifloxacin, Ceftriaxone (biliary elimination), Azithromycin.

Always check the current product information or local formulary for specific guidance.
Consult pharmacy for patients with eGFR < 30 mL/min receiving renally excreted antibiotics.

## Q4: Which antibiotics and drugs are safe to use in pregnancy?

**A:** Drug safety in pregnancy is categorised by trimester. General principles:

**Generally considered safe throughout pregnancy:**
- **Amoxicillin**, **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** — most widely used antibiotics in
  pregnancy; appropriate for UTI, chest infections, skin infections.
- **Cefalexin**, **Ceftriaxone** — first and third-generation cephalosporins considered
  safe; commonly used for UTI and pyelonephritis in pregnancy.
- **Erythromycin** (base or estolate-free preparations) — used for penicillin-allergic
  patients, Chlamydia treatment, and CAP.
- **Nitrofurantoin** — safe in second trimester; **avoid near term (≥ 36 weeks)** due
  to risk of neonatal haemolytic anaemia.

**Avoid in pregnancy:**
- **Tetracyclines (doxycycline, tetracycline):** Bone and tooth discolouration;
  impaired bone growth in fetus. **Contraindicated throughout pregnancy.**
- **Trimethoprim:** Folate antagonist — **avoid in first trimester**; associated with
  neural tube defects. Risk lower in second/third trimester but alternatives preferred.
- **Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin):** Theoretical risk of cartilage
  damage; generally avoided. Use only when no alternative exists.
- **Metronidazole (high-dose):** Avoid in first trimester; lower-dose oral use in
  second/third trimester generally considered acceptable for Trichomonas or C. diff.
- **Chloramphenicol:** Grey baby syndrome — **avoid near term.**

For any antibiotic in pregnancy, consult local obstetric guidelines and discuss
with the obstetric team.

## Q5: What blood tests are required to monitor patients on common antibiotics?

**A:** Monitoring requirements vary by drug and duration of treatment.

**Vancomycin:**
- Renal function (urea, creatinine, eGFR) at baseline and every 48–72 hours or with
  dose change.
- Vancomycin AUC/MIC monitoring (preferred) or trough levels (target 15–20 mg/L).
- FBC if prolonged course (> 2 weeks) — neutropenia risk.

**Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin):**
- Renal function and drug levels with every dose initially (once-daily extended-interval
  dosing: pre-dose level at 16–24 h; target < 1 mg/L for gentamicin).
- Audiology referral for courses > 10 days.

**Linezolid (prolonged courses > 2 weeks):**
- FBC weekly — thrombocytopenia, anaemia, neutropenia.
- Optic neuropathy monitoring (visual acuity) for courses > 28 days.

**Metronidazole (prolonged courses > 4 weeks):**
- Monitor for peripheral neuropathy symptoms (paraesthesiae, distal limb tingling).
- LFTs — mild elevation possible.

**Antifungals (fluconazole, voriconazole — systemic):**
- LFTs at baseline and weekly.
- QTc interval (ECG) for drugs with QT-prolonging potential (voriconazole, posaconazole).
- Voriconazole levels if poor response or toxicity suspected.

**Ciprofloxacin / Fluoroquinolones:**
- ECG if QT-prolonging co-medications or cardiac risk factors (QTc prolongation risk).
- No routine blood monitoring required for short courses in patients with normal renal
  function.

## Q6: What is the appropriate treatment duration for common infections?

**A:** Evidence-based treatment durations for common infectious conditions:

| Infection | Standard duration | Comments |
|---|---|---|
| Uncomplicated cystitis (women) | 3–5 days | Nitrofurantoin 5 days; trimethoprim 7 days |
| Pyelonephritis (uncomplicated) | 7–14 days | 7 days if rapid clinical response |
| Community-acquired pneumonia (mild) | 5 days | Extend to 7 if slow response |
| Community-acquired pneumonia (severe) | 7–10 days | 14 days for Legionella |
| Hospital-acquired pneumonia | 7–8 days | 14 days for Pseudomonas or Acinetobacter |
| Cellulitis | 5–7 days | Reassess if not improving at 48 h |
| Septic arthritis (native joint) | 4–6 weeks | IV for at least 2 weeks |
| Osteomyelitis | 6 weeks total | Specialist input required |
| Infective endocarditis (viridans strep) | 4 weeks | 2-week regimens for selected low-risk cases |
| Infective endocarditis (Staph aureus) | 6 weeks | Native valve; IV therapy throughout |

## Q7: What are the signs and management of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea and C. difficile?

**A:**

**Antibiotic-associated diarrhoea (AAD):**
- Occurs in 5–25% of patients receiving antibiotics; most resolves when antibiotics stop.
- Usually mild watery diarrhoea without systemic features.
- Management: hydration, stop or switch the antibiotic if clinically possible.
- **Always send stool for C. difficile toxin if diarrhoea is persistent (> 3 loose
  stools in 24 h), profuse, or accompanied by systemic features.**

**Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI):**
- **Symptoms:** Watery diarrhoea (≥ 3 loose stools in 24 h), abdominal cramps,
  fever, leukocytosis; in severe cases: ileus, toxic megacolon, septic shock.
- **Diagnosis:** Stool C. difficile glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH) antigen AND toxin
  immunoassay; PCR if equivocal.
- **Treatment:**
  - Stop precipitating antibiotic if possible.
  - **First episode:** Fidaxomicin 200 mg orally BD for 10 days (preferred) or
    Vancomycin 125 mg orally QDS for 10 days.
  - **Recurrent CDI:** Fidaxomicin; or bezlotoxumab adjunctive therapy; consider
    faecal microbiota transplant for multiply recurrent cases.
- **Infection control:** Strict contact precautions; alcohol gel ineffective —
  use soap and water hand hygiene.

## Q8: When should I consider antifungal prophylaxis or empirical antifungal therapy?

**A:**

**Antifungal prophylaxis** is recommended in selected high-risk groups:
- Haematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) recipients during neutropenia:
  **Fluconazole** 400 mg OD or **Posaconazole** 300 mg OD (if mould-active cover
  required for high-risk patients).
- Solid organ transplant recipients: centre-specific protocols; commonly
  fluconazole for 3–6 months.
- Patients receiving intensive immunosuppression for haematological malignancy:
  per local protocol.

**Empirical antifungal therapy** should be considered when:
- Neutropenic patient with persistent fever > 4 days despite broad-spectrum
  antibiotics.
- Clinical or radiological signs suggestive of invasive fungal infection
  (halo sign on CT, sinusitis with bone destruction).
- Proven fungal infection elsewhere in immunosuppressed host.

**Candida:** Echinocandins (caspofungin, micafungin, anidulafungin) are first-line
for candidaemia and invasive candidiasis. Fluconazole is appropriate for non-critical
patients with fluconazole-sensitive Candida.

**Aspergillus:** Voriconazole is first-line for invasive aspergillosis; monitor
levels. Isavuconazole is an alternative with fewer drug interactions.

## Q9: What is the recommended approach to sepsis antibiotic prescribing?

**A:** Sepsis is a medical emergency. The Surviving Sepsis Campaign recommends the
following approach to antibiotic prescribing:

**Time to antibiotics:**
- **Septic shock:** Administer intravenous antibiotics **within 1 hour** of
  recognition. Delay beyond 1 hour is associated with significantly increased mortality.
- **Sepsis without shock:** Target **within 3 hours**.

**Broad-spectrum empirical coverage:**
- Start broad and de-escalate based on culture results at 48–72 hours.
- **Community-acquired sepsis (unknown source):**
  - **Piperacillin-tazobactam** 4.5 g IV every 6 hours, OR
  - **Co-amoxiclav** 1.2 g IV every 8 hours + **Gentamicin** 5 mg/kg IV OD
    (if severe or immunosuppressed)
- **Hospital-acquired sepsis / healthcare-associated:**
  - Add MRSA cover (vancomycin) if risk factors present.
  - Consider Pseudomonas cover (piperacillin-tazobactam or meropenem) in
    patients with structural lung disease, recent broad-spectrum antibiotics,
    or ICU-acquired infection.

**Cultures before antibiotics:**
- Obtain **blood cultures × 2** (from separate sites) before starting antibiotics
  if this does not cause delay beyond 45 minutes.
- Also send cultures from the suspected source (urine, sputum, wound swab).

**Source control:**
- Identify and treat the source of infection urgently — drain abscess, remove
  infected catheter, refer for surgical debridement where indicated.

## Q10: What are the key drug monitoring requirements for patients on long-term antiepileptic medication?

**A:** Long-term antiepileptic drug (AED) monitoring includes both clinical
assessment and laboratory monitoring.

**Phenytoin:**
- Narrow therapeutic index — measure plasma levels if toxicity suspected or
  treatment failure.
- Target total phenytoin: 40–80 µmol/L (10–20 mg/L); free phenytoin 4–8 µg/mL.
- Interactions common (CYP inducers/inhibitors): check for interactions with any
  new medication.
- LFTs annually; FBC annually.

**Valproate:**
- Target level: 40–100 mg/L (not always reliable — dose by clinical response).
- LFTs and FBC at baseline and 6-monthly; more frequently if symptoms suggest hepatotoxicity.
- **Teratogenicity:** Valproate is highly teratogenic — absolute contraindication in
  women of childbearing potential unless Pregnancy Prevention Programme in place.
- Weight gain and hair loss common — counsel patients.

**Carbamazepine:**
- Target level: 17–42 µmol/L (4–10 mg/L); measure at steady state (after 2–4 weeks).
- FBC and LFTs at baseline and 6-monthly — rare but serious aplastic anaemia and
  hepatotoxicity.
- Hyponatraemia: measure sodium at baseline and after dose changes; especially
  relevant in elderly.
- Multiple drug interactions: CYP3A4 inducer — check all co-medications.

**Levetiracetam:**
- No routine plasma level monitoring required.
- Monitor for neuropsychiatric adverse effects (irritability, depression, psychosis)
  — especially important in adolescents.
- Dose reduction required in renal impairment.

**Lamotrigine:**
- Therapeutic drug monitoring not routinely required; consider in pregnancy,
  suspected non-adherence, or lack of efficacy.
- Serious skin reactions (Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis) —
  slow dose titration reduces risk; counsel patients.
- Valproate co-administration approximately doubles lamotrigine levels — halve
  lamotrigine dose.
